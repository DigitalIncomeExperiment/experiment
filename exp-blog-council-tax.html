How I Got a £1,500 Council Tax Refund — and How to Check if You're Owed One Too

I was in the wrong council tax band for years without knowing. Here is exactly what I did about it, what happened, and how you can check yours in under 10 minutes.

This one does not fit neatly into any of the nine income stream experiments. It is not a side hustle. It is not passive income. It is something simpler and in some ways more satisfying — money that was already mine, that I had been overpaying for years without realising.

The council tax refund was a product of exactly that mindset.

"I was in the wrong council tax band for years. The system does not automatically correct it. You have to spot it yourself and challenge it."

How Council Tax Banding Works

Every property in England, Scotland and Wales is assigned a council tax band — from A (lowest) to H (highest). These bands were set in 1991, based on estimated property values at that time. The critical word there is estimated. The Valuation Office Agency (VOA) did not survey every property individually in 1991. They made assumptions, and some of those assumptions were wrong.

Your council tax band determines how much you pay each year. If your band is too high, you have been overpaying — and because bands were set back in 1991, if your property has been in the wrong band since then, that overpayment stretches back a long way.

The system does not automatically fix mistakes. Your council will not write to you and say you have been in the wrong band. You have to identify it yourself and make a formal challenge.

The Two Checks You Must Do First

Before you challenge anything, you need to pass two checks. I only submitted my challenge after both came back positive. This is important — if you only do one and the evidence is thin, you could end up in a worse position. Do both.

Check 1 — The Neighbours Check

Go to gov.uk/council-tax-bands and enter your postcode. You will see your own band and every other property nearby. Look at similar properties on your street — same size, same age, same type. If comparable homes are in a lower band, you may have a case.

When I did this I looked at around 10 to 11 nearby properties to build a solid picture. One or two comparisons is not enough — you want a clear pattern. Make a list of the addresses and their bands. These become your evidence.

Important: A challenge can result in your band going up as well as down. There have been cases where a resident challenged their band and their neighbours ended up being upbanded instead. Only proceed if the comparable evidence is clear and consistent.

Check 2 — The 1991 Valuation Check

Bands were assigned based on 1991 property values. To verify your case, you need to estimate what your property would have been worth in 1991. This is used as supporting evidence rather than definitive proof, but it is an important part of building a complete picture.

The process involves finding your property's most recent sale price, using a free house price tool such as Zoopla, Rightmove or Nethouseprices to find comparable sales, and then working backwards to estimate what the property was worth in 1991. To do the actual calculation I used the Nationwide House Price Index calculator. You enter a known sale price and date and it estimates what the property was worth at any given point in time. Nationwide are clear it is a guide rather than a precise valuation — but it was enough to confirm my property sat within Band C territory in 1991, not Band D.

How to Submit Your Challenge

1

Get your evidence together

List the comparable properties you have found — addresses, band, and a brief description of why they are comparable (size, type, same street). Include your 1991 valuation estimate as supporting evidence.

2

Write your challenge letter

Submit a formal request to the VOA referencing your comparable properties. We have created a free template you can copy and adapt — get it here. No solicitor needed.

3

Submit and be patient — this takes time

You can submit online at gov.uk/challenge-council-tax-band or by post. Then wait. My challenge took over two years from submission to final decision. That is not unusual. Submit as soon as possible and be prepared for a long process.

4

Choose your refund option

If your challenge is successful, you will typically be offered a credit against future bills. But you can also request a full cash refund. I called the council directly after the decision and asked for the full amount paid back. It was agreed on the call and the money was in my account within two weeks.

Is It Worth Doing?

Yes — emphatically. The process is free, the potential return is significant and the only real cost is time. The fact that it can take two years or more is the main reason to get your challenge in as soon as possible. The sooner you submit, the sooner the clock starts.

Compare it to selling digital products, running affiliate links or completing surveys. Hours of ongoing work for modest returns. A successful council tax challenge recovers money you had already handed over, with no ongoing effort required once the challenge is submitted.
