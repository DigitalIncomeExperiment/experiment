After the decision: how to get your council tax refund in cash

March 2026·Experiment 08·Follow-up to the original post

The VOA upheld the challenge. Band D became Band C. But what happens next is something most guides don’t cover — specifically, the difference between a credit on your account and actual cash in your bank. Here’s exactly what we did, and what you should ask for.

Quick recap

If you haven’t read the original council tax post, the short version: after checking comparable properties on our street and estimating the 1991 value using the Nationwide House Price Index calculator, a formal challenge was submitted to the Valuation Office Agency. The process took over two years. The decision came back in our favour — Band D reduced to Band C.

When the decision lands, most councils automatically apply the overpayment as a credit to your council tax account. This means future bills are reduced until the credit is used up. For most people this is fine — but it’s not your only option, and for a refund of this size, it’s worth knowing what else you can ask for.

Credit vs cash — why it matters

A credit on your council tax account is not the same as money in your bank. It will reduce your future bills, which is useful — but it ties your money to one specific purpose, paid out gradually over months. If you’d rather have the lump sum now, you can ask for it.

“They don’t advertise the cash refund option. You have to ask for it specifically — and it’s a single phone call.”

The council will not proactively offer you a cash refund. In our experience, the default assumption is that a credit suits everyone. It doesn’t — and the right to request a cash refund of overpaid council tax is well established. You are entitled to ask.

How to request the cash refund — step by step

1

Wait for the formal decision letter

The VOA will write to you and your council confirming the band change. Don’t contact the council until this letter has arrived — the council needs to update its records before it can process a refund. In our case this took a few days after the VOA decision.

2

Call your council’s council tax department directly

Don’t use the general enquiries number. Search for your council’s specific council tax team. When you get through, confirm they have received the updated banding from the VOA — ask them to confirm the new band and the credit balance on your account before you ask for the refund.

3

Ask explicitly for a cash refund

The exact phrase that worked for us: “I’d like to request a cash refund of the overpaid amount rather than a credit on my account.” The agent will ask for your bank details to process a BACS transfer. Have your sort code and account number ready.

4

Confirm the amount and timeline

Ask them to confirm the exact refund amount and when it will be paid. In our case the agent confirmed the amount on the call and gave a timeline of up to 10 working days. The money arrived within two weeks.

5

Get a reference number

Before you hang up, ask for a reference number for the refund request. This means if there’s any delay you can follow up with something specific rather than starting from scratch.

How long it actually took: Decision confirmed by VOA → called council the next working day → refund request confirmed on the call → money in the bank within two weeks. No chasing required. One phone call.

The ongoing saving

The refund was a one-off recovery of past overpayments. But the band change is permanent — and the ongoing saving is where the real long-term value sits.

Band D to Band C means a lower multiplier applied to your council’s base rate every year, indefinitely. At current rates that’s approximately £240 per year — every year, for as long as you stay in the property. Over ten years that’s £2,400. Over twenty years, £4,800. The refund was significant. The ongoing saving will eventually dwarf it.

Important: The band change follows the property, not the occupant. If you sell, the new owner benefits. If you move into a property that has already had a successful challenge, you benefit. If you’re a tenant, you can still challenge — but your landlord may be liable for some of the overpayment depending on your agreement. Worth clarifying before you submit.
