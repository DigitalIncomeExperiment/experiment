Every tool this site uses — and what each one costs

March 2026·8 min read

The site URL says github.io. That’s not a mistake or an oversight — it’s a deliberate choice. The entire infrastructure behind the Digital Income Experiment costs nothing to run. Here’s every tool, why it was chosen, and what it costs. The total monthly bill is £0.

Why this matters — and why it’s deliberate

The Experiment is testing whether online income is real. That premise breaks immediately if the infrastructure required to document it costs more than the income it generates. An experiment about making money online that costs £50/month to run before it’s earned a penny isn’t an honest test — it’s a loss-making project in a race to break even.

“The github.io URL isn’t a limitation we’re working around. It’s proof that the premise works. The site exists. It cost nothing to build and costs nothing to run.”

There’s a second reason. A lot of “start an online income” content quietly assumes you already have a website, a Wordpress subscription, a paid email platform, a domain, maybe a Canva Pro account. The total of all those “small” monthly costs is often £40–£80/month before you’ve earned anything. That’s a significant barrier — and it’s worth demonstrating that it doesn’t have to be.

Every tool used in the Experiment was chosen to be free first, capable second. Here’s the full breakdown.

The full stack — every tool, every cost

GitHub Pages
Website hosting

GitHub Pages hosts the entire DIE website — all 33 pages, all assets, all blog posts — completely free. It’s a feature of GitHub (a platform developers use to store and manage code) that publishes static HTML files as a live website. No server to manage. No hosting control panel. No renewal invoices.

Why we use it: It’s genuinely free with no meaningful limitations for a content site. The URL is github.io rather than a custom domain — that’s the only visible sign you’re on a free host. A custom domain costs ~£12/year and can be connected at any time. That will happen when the Experiment has earned enough to justify it — and not before.

Honest limitation: GitHub Pages hosts static files only. No database, no login system, no dynamic content. For a documentation and content site this is no limitation at all. If the Experiment eventually needed a proper web application, the infrastructure would need upgrading. That day hasn’t come.
£0per month
Sender
Email capture & automation

Sender handles all email sign-ups on the site — the embedded forms, the welcome email that delivers the subscriber guide, and the monthly income report distribution. The free plan covers up to 2,500 subscribers and 15,000 emails per month.

Why we use it: Mailchimp, Klaviyo, and ConvertKit all have free tiers but with tighter limits or more aggressive upsell prompts. Sender’s free tier is genuinely generous for a site at this stage. The automation workflow — subscriber signs up, receives welcome email with private guide link — is set up and running on the free plan.

Honest note: The email is currently sent from a Digital Revenue Studio account (info@digitalrevenuestudio.co.uk) rather than a DIE-specific address. This is a temporary arrangement due to email authentication restrictions. It’s documented transparently on every form. A dedicated DIE email account is on the roadmap once the Experiment is self-sustaining.
£0per month
Ko-fi
Shop & voluntary support

Ko-fi hosts the Experiment’s shop — currently the KDP Cash Machine Playbook at £4.99. It also functions as Experiment 09: a voluntary tip jar testing whether honest content generates organic reader support. Ko-fi takes 0% on one-off payments on the free plan.

Why we use it: Zero transaction fee on the free plan is the key differentiator. Gumroad takes 10%. Payhip takes 5%. Ko-fi takes nothing on direct payments (payment processor fees still apply). For a shop at this stage that’s the right call — every penny of each £4.99 sale counts.

£0per month
Google Fonts
Typography

Three typefaces load from Google Fonts: Syne (headings), DM Mono (body text), and Instrument Serif (italic accents). These are the fonts that give the site its distinctive look — they’re not system defaults.

Why we use it: Professional typography is one of the biggest visual differentiators between a site that looks considered and one that looks like a default template. Google Fonts provides access to high-quality typefaces that would cost hundreds of pounds to license commercially — for free, hosted on Google’s CDN.

£0per month
Amazon Associates
Affiliate programme — Experiment 03

The Amazon affiliate programme generates a small commission when a reader clicks a link from the site and makes a purchase. February confirmed £0.12. March confirmed £0.45. Small, but the model is working and it compounds as content and traffic grow.

Why we use it: Free to join. No monthly fee. Links to products that are genuinely recommended — no paid placement. The Recommended section of the site is the primary home for affiliate links, all clearly disclosed.

£0per month
Amazon KDP
Publishing platform — Experiment 01

Kindle Direct Publishing is free to use. No upfront publishing fee. No listing fee. Amazon takes a royalty cut (30% at the 70% royalty tier) but there’s no cost to publish. The book is live, KDP Select is enrolled, and royalties are accumulating.

Why we use it: There’s no viable free alternative with Amazon’s reach. KDP is the platform. The zero upfront cost is the point — £24.40 confirmed in royalties with no publishing investment beyond time.

£0upfront
Payhip
Digital products — Experiment 02 (DRS)

Payhip hosts the CDM digital product under the DRS brand. Free plan, 5% transaction fee on sales. Zero monthly cost. Listed and waiting — no sales yet from this channel.

Why we use it: Free to list, no monthly fee, simple storefront. The 5% fee is only paid when a sale happens — which is the right model for an experiment at this stage.

£0per month
Freecash, InboxPounds, EarnLabs
Earn by playing — Experiment 04

Three reward platforms tested simultaneously. Free to join. Earn points from completing offers, playing games, and other tasks — redeemable for cash or gift cards. Net profit March: £28.72 (£82.69 earned, £53.97 costs). Still ongoing.

Why we use them: No joining fee. The experiment cost is the in-app purchases required by some offers — that’s the real cost being tracked, not a platform subscription.

£0to join
Quidco & TopCashback
Cashback — Experiment 05

Both major UK cashback platforms, running simultaneously for a head-to-head comparison. Both free to join. First tracked cashback: £1.65 on a Currys hoover purchase via TopCashback at 5.75% (vs Quidco’s 3.75%).

Why we use them: Free to join, earn on spending you’d make anyway. The only “cost” is the mental overhead of checking rates before purchasing. Worth it every time.

£0per month
Amazon Shopper Panel
Surveys — Experiment 06

Amazon’s survey platform. Quick, targeted surveys — better disqualification rate than most alternatives. 19 surveys in March, £5.15 confirmed. Pays in Amazon credit, redeemable as cash equivalent.

Why we use it: Invite-only but free once in. No subscription. The surveys are genuinely quicker than most platforms and the payout rate is honest.

£0per month
Fiverr
Freelancing — Experiment 07

Three gigs live. Zero orders so far — the cold start problem is the experiment. Fiverr takes 20% of each order but there’s no monthly fee to list. The cost of having three gigs live and unordered: exactly £0.

Why we use it: No upfront cost to list, large buyer marketplace, well-known platform. The 20% fee is only relevant when it’s paying on orders that don’t exist yet.

£0per month

What changes when the Experiment earns more

The zero-cost infrastructure is not a permanent ambition — it’s the right starting point. As the Experiment generates confirmed income, infrastructure gets upgraded in a deliberate order:

First (~£12/year): A custom domain. The github.io URL works perfectly well, but a proper domain signals permanence. This happens when the Experiment has earned enough to cover it without dipping into personal funds.

Second: A dedicated DIE email address with its own Sender account. Separates the Experiment from DRS properly and resolves the current email attribution arrangement.

Third (if needed): A hosting upgrade if an experiment requires dynamic content, user accounts, or database functionality. Not needed yet — and may never be needed if the experiment model stays content-led.

The principle: Nothing gets spent until the income justifies it. Every upgrade will be documented in the income reports when it happens — what was spent, why, and what income covered it. The overhead costs of running this Experiment will always be visible.
