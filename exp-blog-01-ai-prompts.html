Using AI as a real working tool — not a shortcut

March 2026 · Experiments 01, 02, 07 · 8 min read

What actually happens when you use AI across publishing, digital product development, and Fiverr gig copy? We tested it properly — with specific prompts, honest assessments, and no hype. Here's what worked, what wasted time, and what the verdict actually is.

The honest starting point

Everyone has an opinion on AI. Half the internet will tell you it's going to replace everything. The other half will tell you it produces garbage that sounds like a corporate brochure written by a committee. Both are wrong — and both are partially right, depending entirely on how you use it.

This post isn't about AI in the abstract. It's about three specific experiments where AI was used as a working tool — not as a content farm, not as a replacement for thinking, but as something closer to a capable assistant that still requires you to know what you're doing.

The three areas tested: publishing (Experiment 01), digital product development (Experiment 02), and Fiverr gig copy (Experiment 07). Different use cases. Different results. All documented here honestly.

"AI doesn't replace the thinking. It accelerates the parts that don't require it."

Experiment 01 — AI and publishing

Publishing a book involves more distinct tasks than most people realise before they start. There's the writing itself, but there's also the research, the structure, the editing, the formatting, the cover brief, the listing copy, the keyword research, and the marketing description. Each of these is a different kind of problem.

Where AI genuinely helped

Keyword research and listing copy. Amazon KDP is a search-driven marketplace. Getting your book in front of the right readers means understanding how they search. AI was used to generate keyword variations, test different positioning angles, and draft the listing description. The quality of the output was directly proportional to the quality of the brief given — vague prompts produced generic copy, specific prompts produced something usable.

Prompt that worked
"You are an Amazon KDP specialist. Write a book listing description for a children's picture book — rhyming, ages 3–7, hide-and-seek theme, dog character. The description should be warm and engaging, front-load the core appeal, and include the key search terms naturally without keyword stuffing. Max 400 characters."
Result: Three strong draft options in under 30 seconds. Edited one of them. Went live. This is the kind of task AI is genuinely good at — constrained, defined, goal-clear.
Prompt that wasted time
"Write me a children's book about a dog."
Result: Generically pleasant nonsense with no character, no rhythm, no soul. The output was not unusable — it was just not the book. AI cannot supply the creative vision. It can execute against one.

Where AI didn't help

The writing itself. This is where the nuance matters. AI can produce text that has the surface features of a children's book — rhyme schemes, simple vocabulary, short sentences. What it cannot produce is the specific creative instinct that makes a book worth reading: the precise word that lands right, the rhythm that children want to repeat, the joke that earns the laugh. Those require a human who understands what they're making.

Used AI to generate structural scaffolding and test rhyme options against each other. Wrote the actual book content. That split — AI for structure and iteration, human for the actual creative work — produced the best results.

✓ Verdict — Worth using for publishing

Listing copy, keyword research, formatting guidelines, category research — all strong AI use cases. The actual creative content requires a human. Anyone telling you AI can write a publishable book without significant human involvement hasn't read the output carefully enough.

Experiment 02 — AI and digital product development

Building a digital product — a tool, a template, a system — is a different kind of problem. The creative challenge isn't writing, it's architecture: deciding what the product actually does, what problem it solves, how it's structured, and whether it's genuinely useful or just another PDF someone downloads and forgets.

Scoping and structuring

The CDM (Content Debt Management) tool started as a rough idea and a set of scattered notes. AI was used to stress-test the concept, generate potential objections, and structure the final deliverable. The most useful prompt category: asking AI to be sceptical.

Prompt that worked
"Here is a description of a digital product I am developing for content teams: [description]. Act as a sceptical potential buyer. What are the 5 most likely reasons someone would look at this and not buy? Be specific and honest — not encouraging."
Result: Five sharp, genuinely useful objections. Two of them identified real gaps in the product that were fixed before listing. This is AI as a thinking partner rather than a content generator — and it's where the ROI is highest.

Pricing research

Pricing a digital product without an existing audience to test against is a genuine challenge. AI was used to model different pricing scenarios, identify comparable products, and reason through the tradeoffs between a low entry price (more volume, lower friction) and a higher price (fewer buyers, higher perceived value). The reasoning was sound. The specific market data needed verifying independently — AI's training data has a cutoff and pricing landscapes shift.

→ Verdict — Strong for scoping, weaker for market data

AI is excellent for stress-testing concepts, generating objections, and structuring digital products. Treat its specific market intelligence (pricing benchmarks, platform data, current trends) with scepticism and verify independently. The thinking partner use case is the real value here.

Experiment 07 — AI and Fiverr gig copy

Fiverr is a competitive, search-driven marketplace. Gig titles, descriptions, and tags all affect where you appear in search results. For a new seller with zero reviews, gig copy is one of the few levers available — you can't out-review established sellers, but you can write better than them.

The cold start problem

Three gigs were listed. For each one, AI was used to: generate gig title variations, test different positioning angles, draft and refine the description, and identify high-volume low-competition tags. The prompt framework that produced the best results was structured around Fiverr's specific algorithm priorities, not just general copywriting.

Prompt framework that worked
"You are an expert in Fiverr SEO and gig optimisation. I am a new seller with zero reviews offering [service]. Write 5 gig title variations that: 1) lead with the buyer's outcome not the service, 2) are under 80 characters, 3) include the primary keyword naturally, 4) differentiate from the generic titles already ranking. Service description: [description]."
Result: Title options that were measurably better than the first draft. Impressions increased after the update. Zero orders yet — but the gig is visible in search, which it wasn't before.

What AI can't fix

The core challenge on Fiverr for a new seller isn't copy quality — it's the algorithm's preference for sellers who already have orders and reviews. No amount of optimised gig copy overcomes the cold start problem entirely. AI can help you write the best possible listing. It cannot substitute for social proof that doesn't exist yet.

· Verdict — Useful but not the bottleneck

AI produced genuinely better gig copy than the first draft. The impression rate improved. The bottleneck is still the zero-review cold start. Optimised copy is a necessary but not sufficient condition for getting the first order on Fiverr.

The honest overall verdict

AI is a productivity tool, not a creativity replacement. The pattern across all three experiments was consistent: the more defined and constrained the task, the better the output. The more the task required genuine creative judgment — knowing what makes something good, not just what makes it technically correct — the less useful AI was on its own.

The most useful reframe: stop thinking of AI as something that produces finished outputs. Start thinking of it as something that dramatically speeds up the distance between a blank page and a workable first draft — and as a thinking partner that can stress-test ideas, generate objections, and produce variations quickly.

→ The principle that held across everything

Prompt quality determines output quality. Vague brief → generic output. Specific brief with context, constraints, and a clear goal → something genuinely useful. The skill isn't using AI. It's knowing what to ask for and recognising when the output is actually good versus when it merely looks good.
