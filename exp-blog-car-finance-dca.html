Car Finance DCA — What I Did, What My Lenders Confirmed and What It Actually Means

I wrote to three car finance lenders in July 2024. All three confirmed DCAs were attached. Here is my honest account — including what having a DCA confirmed actually does and does not mean for your chances of redress.

Before I start — Martin Lewis and MoneySavingExpert have covered car finance DCAs extensively and deserve full credit for raising awareness of this. I came across it through my own research, but his site is the most comprehensive resource available and worth reading alongside this post.

What I can add is my own first-hand experience of going through the process myself. This is not a financial briefing or a guarantee of compensation. It is an honest account of what I did, what happened and — crucially — what having a DCA confirmed actually does and does not mean. That last part is something most guides gloss over.

"Having a DCA confirmed does not automatically mean compensation is coming. The type of DCA matters. Most guides do not make this clear enough."

The Three Types of DCA — This Is What Most Guides Miss

This is the part worth reading carefully before you do anything else. Discretionary commission arrangements came in three forms, and only one of them is likely to result in redress. Understanding which type applies to your agreement matters before you get your hopes up or dismiss the process entirely.

Negative DCA

The broker or dealer used the arrangement to increase your interest rate in order to earn a higher commission. You paid more than you should have. This is the type the FCA review is primarily concerned with.

Likely outcome: Redress possible
Neutral DCA

A DCA was present on the agreement but was not used to change your rate. Your interest rate was not affected. You were not financially disadvantaged by it.

Likely outcome: No compensation
Positive DCA

The arrangement was used to lower your interest rate. The dealer may have accepted a lower commission to close the deal. You benefited from a better rate than you might otherwise have received.

Likely outcome: No compensation

The letter I used — and the template on this site — asks your lender to confirm whether any DCA was present. Once they respond, the nature of that DCA determines whether you have a strong case. If they confirm a negative DCA, you are in the right territory. If they confirm neutral or positive, you are unlikely to see redress — but it is still worth having the confirmation in writing.

How I Found Out

I came across a short video on Facebook about discretionary commission arrangements on car finance. Rather than take it at face value I went and did my own research. I had three finance agreements that all fell within the relevant window — all taken out before January 2021 — so I decided to act.

In July 2024 I wrote directly to all three lenders myself rather than using a third party claims company. Claims companies take a significant percentage of any redress — doing it yourself means you keep everything.

What I Did and What Happened

The letter was straightforward. I asked each lender whether a discretionary commission arrangement had been attached to my agreement and, if confirmed, requested that my letter be treated as a formal complaint. I sent it by email to keep a timestamped record.

My three lenders were Black Horse, MotoNovo and Mann Island. All three responded in writing confirming that DCAs had been attached to my agreements. Importantly — in my case these were confirmed as negative DCAs. My rates had been set to their disadvantage. That distinction matters, as explained above.

The Wait — What the Pause Actually Means

The FCA has paused the requirement for lenders to respond to complaints while a redress scheme is being finalised. This means I am currently waiting alongside millions of others for the scheme to be confirmed and for lenders to be told how to process claims.

The pause is not a dismissal. If you have submitted a complaint and received acknowledgement, your complaint is in the queue. The mechanism for paying out is still being decided — that is all the pause means.

On compensation figures: Various figures have circulated in the media about likely average payouts. None of these are confirmed. The FCA redress scheme has not been finalised and until it is, no one — including the lenders themselves — knows what individual payouts will look like. This post will be updated when confirmed figures are available.

What to Do If You Have Not Checked Yet

1

Check your agreement date

Agreements taken out before 28 January 2021 fall within the FCA review window. After that date you are outside the scope.

2

Find your lender

Check old finance documents or your credit file. Common lenders include Black Horse, MotoNovo, Mann Island, Close Brothers and Santander Consumer Finance.

3

Write to the lender directly

Ask whether a DCA was attached and request it be treated as a formal complaint if confirmed. The letter template I used is available free on this site — no sign-up needed. Get the template here.

4

Keep everything in writing

Send by email and keep copies of everything including acknowledgement responses. If they confirm a DCA was attached, note whether they specify the type.

5

Do not use a claims company

This process is straightforward and completely free to do yourself. A claims company will take a significant percentage of any redress you receive. There is no need to use one.
